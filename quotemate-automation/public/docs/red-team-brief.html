We attack our own platform before the market does

Five ways QuoteMate could be gamed, mishandled or doubted.

For each one: exactly what we’ve built, what we’re building, and what we’ve deliberately chosen. Gaps included — because a control you can’t name yet isn’t a control you can trust.

How to read this brief

Every control carries an honest status

The assessment

Five threat vectors, answered in the open

01
TV-01
Threat level
High

A tradie takes the job off-platform

The risk

A tradie wins work through QuoteMate, marks it lost or cancelled, then quietly settles with the customer in private to dodge the platform fee.

Our controls
  • Outcome capture. Every quote carries a status and a routing decision. Cancelled and not-won jobs are recorded at the data layer — the raw signal a bypass leaves behind. Live
  • Loss-rate anomaly report. A periodic report flags any tradie whose cancelled / not-won pattern breaks from the norm for their trade and volume. Roadmap
  • Threshold suspension. Accounts that cross the abuse threshold are flagged and can be suspended pending review. By design
Honest note

The platform fee is secured inside the customer deposit (see TV-02), so even one off-platform job still leaves our fee paid. Programmatic enforcement — the automated report and auto-suspension — depends on the funds layer, which runs in Stripe test mode today. The data to build the report is already being captured; the report and the block are the next step, not a finished feature.

02
TV-02
Threat level
Low

The customer wants to pay cash

The risk

The customer settles the balance in cash, so the platform appears to see none of the money that changed hands.

Our position
  • We take our cut from the deposit, not the invoice. The deposit is paid digitally before work starts; our share comes out of it. Once the deposit clears, we’re paid. By design
  • The balance is the tradie’s business. Card, bank transfer or cash for the remainder is between the tradie and their customer. Cash is genuinely fine — it doesn’t touch our fee. By design
  • Per-tier deposit checkout. Good / Better / Best each carry a deposit link, plus the $99 inspection path, running through Stripe checkout. Live
Honest note

Deposits run through Stripe test keys today, and the Connect Express funds-split that routes our fee to us automatically is not yet wired — no live fee has moved in production. The model is proven end-to-end in test mode; switching it to real money is the milestone, not the design.

03
TV-03
Threat level
Medium

Tradies are too hard to onboard

The risk

A sparky abandons setup before their first quote ever drafts, so they never reach the moment that proves the value.

Our controls
  • Self-serve onboarding. Sign up, walk through onboarding, go live — no spreadsheet handover and no waiting on us. Live
  • Automated provisioning. Each tradie’s SMS number and voice assistant are stood up automatically as part of activation. In build
  • Guided onboarding workshops. For tradies who’d rather be walked through it, a hands-on session gets their pricing book dialled in. By design
Honest note

The onboarding pages are live and usable. The Twilio and Vapi auto-provisioning code is written but currently sits behind feature flags that are off in dev — numbers and assistants are provisioned by hand until those flags flip on.

04
TV-04
Threat level
High

The estimate is wrong

The risk

An invented or mis-priced line item goes out under the tradie’s name — eroding trust and creating real liability. This is the vector we’ve hardened the most.

Our controls — this is what a price-checking agent and an estimate-validation agent look like in production
  • Prices are tool-call only. The model can never free-type a number. Every price is fetched from the pricing book by a tool — not generated. Live
  • Grounding validator. Every line item is checked: each price must derive from the tradie’s pricing book, the shared assemblies and materials, scoped to that exact trade. Live
  • Safe failure. If a single price can’t be grounded, the whole quote is downgraded to a $99 human inspection rather than guessed. The fallback is the backstop. Live
  • Confidence routing & precedent. A confidence router and retrieval over similar past jobs keep every draft anchored to real, comparable work. Live
  • Formal eval rubric. 100 hold-out intake→quote pairs, scored across five dimensions, to measure accuracy deltas between prompt versions. Roadmap
Honest note

The grounding backstop is live and runs on every single quote — that is the part that protects the tradie today. The formal eval harness that scores accuracy over a hold-out set isn’t built yet; a 70-assertion parity harness guards against regressions in the meantime. We iterate prompts with a safety net, not yet with a scoreboard.

05
TV-05
Threat level
Medium

“Only I can price this job”

The risk

The owner believes their pricing judgment can’t be matched by software, so they never trust the draft — and the product never gets a fair run.

Our controls
  • It runs on their numbers, not ours. The estimate uses the owner’s own hourly rate, markup, custom assemblies and preferred materials — built with them, not imposed on them. Live
  • Same inputs, same answer, every time. The consistency a busy owner can’t hold in their head at 9pm after a full day on the tools. Live
  • It doesn’t bluff. When a job is genuinely ambiguous, the system books the $99 inspection — exactly the call the owner would make. Live
The proof

Put a job the owner knows cold through the draft. It returns their price, in seconds, grounded in their own book — and books an inspection when it shouldn’t guess. That is the demo that changes the conversation from “only I can do this” to “this does what I do, faster.”

The standard we hold ourselves to

Transparency isn’t a slide. It’s how the codebase is documented.

QuoteMate ships with its gaps written down, not painted over. Where a control is a stance, we say so. Where it’s on the roadmap, we say so. Where money hasn’t moved in production yet, we say that too. The same honesty you see in this brief is the honesty our engineering notes are written with — drift from a plan is called out explicitly rather than hidden.
