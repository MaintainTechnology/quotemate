The Price Checker is the load-bearing safety layer. It reads every line of the AI's draft quote and confirms each price traces back to a real entry in either shared_assemblies or tenant_custom_assemblies. If even one line can't be matched, the whole quote is replaced with the $99 inspection route. That's why the cookbooks can have gaps and the engine still stays safe — the gate is downstream of the cookbook.

Verdict — the engine is sound

A wrong price can't reach a customer. Worst case is a $99 site visit instead of the auto-quote. The guarantee lives in the Price Checker, not in the cookbooks themselves.

02The downlight gap — you're right

There's only ONE downlight entry in the cookbook (shared_assemblies, row name "Install LED downlight"), and its labour time only fits the "replace existing" job. A "new install" downlight job dumps to a $99 site visit — safe, but a missed quote we could've delivered. Your example walked into this directly.

NEW INSTALL (your first scenario)

  • Find a power source
  • Run new cables through the ceiling
  • Cut holes in the plaster
  • Terminate cables at each fitting
  • Connect downlights
  • Test and commission
Labour: 1.5–2 hours per fitting

REPLACE EXISTING (your second scenario)

  • Remove old downlights
  • Install new terminals
  • Install new downlights
  • Test
Labour: about 25 minutes per fitting

New install is roughly 3–4× the labour per fitting. The existing downlight row in shared_assemblies carries the replacement labour (0.40 hr) and its default_exclusions column literally says "Excludes new wiring runs and ceiling repair" — so by design the row only covers the replacement scenario. Power points got this right: three entries in shared_assemblies for three labour profiles. Downlights and smoke alarms didn't.

Add a second row to shared_assemblies — "Install LED downlight (new install, single-storey, switch within 5m)" at 1.5–2 hours per fitting, with rules to escalate the tricky cases (raked ceiling, no roof access, multi-storey) to a site visit. The easy 80% of new-install jobs auto-quote; the hard 20% still go to a site visit, which is the right call. Same pattern for smoke alarms.

03Where the assumptions live

You mentioned "we can find a switch within 5 metres, we have roof space access, we can drill through framing." Those assumptions DO exist in the system — but spread across three places. The most useful spot (right on the cookbook entry itself) is still missing.

1. Built into the AI's rules
Per job type, default assumptions are baked in. Downlights = flat plaster, indoor, modern house. Used silently when the customer doesn't say otherwise.
ASSUMPTION_RULES in lib/sms/assumptions.ts
2. One line per entry
Each cookbook entry can carry a single short exclusion line. The downlight row's text: "Excludes new wiring runs and ceiling repair." Plain text, not structured.
shared_assemblies.default_exclusions
3. Saved with each quote
When the AI builds a quote, the assumptions it relied on are saved with that quote. The customer sees them and can correct anything wrong.
quotes.assumptions (jsonb array)

What's missing — structured rules ON each entry

Today the downlight row in shared_assemblies only has the one-line exclusion text. It can't say things like "switch must be within 5 metres" because there's no column for structured rules. Adding a new column (shared_assemblies.row_assumptions, a jsonb field) would close this gap. Your example becomes the template:

What "Install LED downlight" carries today

  • One line of exclusion text (default_exclusions)
  • Four must-ask questions (clarifying_questions)
  • No structured rules attached

What it could carry (your example)

  • Switch must be within 5 metres
  • Must have roof space access
  • Flat plaster ceiling only
  • Single-storey only
  • Plus the existing exclusions and questions
Verdict — half there

The first two layers work. The missing piece is per-entry structured assumptions on each shared_assemblies row — the exact thing you described. This pairs with the downlight fix from section 02: add the column once, and every new entry (including the "new install" downlight) can use it.

05Two books, different detail — what happens?

You raised: "there will be detail in one book and not the other. Some tradies just give a one-line invoice." Real concern. The good news: the Price Checker only cares that each line traces back to a real entry somewhere — either book counts. The two books mix together coherently.

Rich master cookbook
Full labour breakdowns, exclusions, materials. We curate this.
shared_assemblies + shared_materials
Sparse tradie's cookbook
Maybe just one entry — "MY Clipsal Iconic GPO at $85". No labour breakdown. That's fine; the rest comes from the master.
tenant_custom_assemblies + tenant_material_catalogue
AI mixes them together
Labour from the master cookbook + part price from the tradie's cookbook = one coherent quote.
Price Checker
Each line just needs to trace to a real entry. Doesn't matter which book.
lib/estimate/validate.ts
Customer sees one clean quote

Worked example with real numbers

Atomic Electrical has uploaded just ONE thing to their personal cookbook (tenant_material_catalogue) — their preferred Clipsal Iconic GPO at $85. A customer asks for a power point replacement.

How the quote gets built
AI looks up labour:"Replace double GPO" — found in shared_assemblies (master). 18 min × $160/hr = $48.
AI looks up part:Atomic's tenant_material_catalogue has Clipsal Iconic at $85. Master has generic at $25. Atomic's wins.
AI builds line:"Replace double GPO with Clipsal Iconic" — labour $48 + part $85 + markup ≈ $175 per GPO.
Price Checker:Labour traces to master ✓. Part traces to tenant ✓. Quote approved.
Customer:Sees the Clipsal Iconic brand named on the quote at Atomic's premium price.
Verdict — already handled by design

A tradie with a rich tenant_custom_assemblies + tenant_material_catalogue gets their brands and prices honoured. A tradie with nothing in those tables falls back to the master — still produces a valid, professional quote. The difference never reaches the customer. Your concern was the right one to raise; the answer is the system was built knowing this would happen.
